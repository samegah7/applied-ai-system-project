Ambient music prioritizes atmosphere and texture over melody, rhythm, or song structure. It originated with Brian Eno's experiments in the late 1970s and is designed to be listened to passively — to shape a sonic environment rather than demand attention. There are usually no vocals, minimal percussion, and very slow harmonic movement.

Typical energy range: very low to low (0.1–0.4). Tempos are often absent or so slow as to be imperceptible. Sounds blend and fade rather than cutting or building. Dynamics are gentle; there are no drops or climactic moments.

Ambient pairs naturally with relaxed, chill, and focused moods — and especially with activities like meditation, sleep, deep work, or spatial design. A user who requests ambient music is almost always looking for the lowest-intensity option available.

When recommending ambient music, the energy match is the most important factor. An ambient song for an energy-0.0 or energy-0.1 user should feel as close to silence as possible while still being music. The mood label matters less than the raw energy level: even a "moody" ambient track is still extremely low-energy and would likely satisfy a relaxation-seeking user better than a low-energy pop track labeled "happy."
